Confidentiality & When to Breach It ‚ñº

GMC Guidance, Public Safety

The patient has a legal duty to inform DVLA, not the doctor. Advise your patient of this, and document that advice clearly in the notes.

You must inform the DVLA directly if:

  • Your patient has a condition that may affect safe driving and they cannot or will not notify DVLA themselves.
  • You believe failure to do so would expose the public to a risk of death or serious harm (as per GMC guidance).

You should, if safe and practicable, inform the patient of your intention to notify DVLA before doing so. You can also contact the DVLA's medical adviser for confidential advice.

Sudden Disabling Events ‚ñº

Risk Thresholds

A driver must not drive if they have a condition likely to cause a sudden disabling event at the wheel.

DVLA defines this risk as a:

  • 20% likelihood of an event in 1 year for Group 1 (Car/Motorcycle).
  • 2% likelihood of an event in 1 year for Group 2 (Bus/Lorry).

This definition is a crucial foundation for all the standards you see below. The stricter standard for Group 2 reflects the higher risk associated with larger vehicles and the nature of occupational driving.

Temporary Conditions ‚ñº

Post-op, Fractures

Generally, drivers do not need to notify DVLA of conditions that are not expected to continue beyond 3 months.

Common examples from the ED include short-term post-operative recovery, minor fractures, or severe migraine. Always advise the patient to check with their insurer.

Neurological Disorders ‚ñº

TLoC, Seizures, SAH

New consolidated Appendix D for Transient Loss of Consciousness (TLoC), including blackouts, with or without seizure markers, and cough syncope.

Guidance revised for Dissociative Seizures and Isolated Seizures with specific timeframes.

The standard for Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH) has been revised to include extradural aneurysms. Functional neurosurgical techniques have a new standard added.

Cardiovascular Disorders ‚ñº

ACS Types, Pacemakers

Standard revised for Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) to explicitly include Type 1 & 2 MI, MINOCA, and Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy.

Pacemaker implant guidance now includes advice for regular review.

The standard for Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillator (ICD) has been revised, with particular clarification for prophylactic implants.

Diabetes Mellitus ‚ñº

Group 2 Glucose Monitoring

Amendment to the duration of blood glucose readings required for insulin-treated Group 2 drivers - must now provide 6 weeks of readings.

A new standard for seizures provoked by hypoglycaemia is included, now requiring a period off driving.

Transient Loss of Consciousness (TLoC) ‚ñº

Unexplained TLoC, Reflex Syncope, Cough Syncope

Unexplained TLoC: G1 drivers must stop driving and notify DVLA for 6 months. For G2, it is 12 months.

Reflex Syncope (Vasovagal): If associated with a reliable prodrome and has not occurred while driving, a G1 driver can continue to drive and need not notify DVLA.

Cough Syncope: A G1 driver must not drive for 6 months following a single episode. A G2 driver must not drive for 12 months.

Seizures ‚ñº

Isolated, Provoked, First Unprovoked

Isolated Seizure: Driving cessation for 6 months (if no underlying cause) or 12 months (if there is one). Must notify DVLA.

Provoked Seizures: Driving usually ceases for 6 months (G1) or up to 5 years (G2). This applies to seizures from acute illness/fever, intracranial bleeds, or severe electrolyte/hypoglycaemia disturbance.

First Unprovoked Seizure: G1 drivers must stop driving for 12 months from the date of the seizure.

Stroke / TIA ‚ñº

Transient Ischaemic Attack, Stroke

TIA: G1 drivers must stop driving for 1 month but need not notify DVLA unless there are residual deficits (e.g., visual or limb impairment).

Stroke: G1 drivers must stop driving. Can resume after 1 month if clinical recovery is satisfactory. Must notify DVLA if there is a residual neurological deficit.

Head Injury ‚ñº

Post-Traumatic Amnesia, Intracranial Haematoma

Driving can resume without notifying DVLA if there is full clinical recovery and no seizures, no post-traumatic amnesia >24 hours, and no intracranial haematoma.

In all other cases, driving is usually prohibited for 6-12 months and DVLA must be notified.

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) ‚ñº

MI, MINOCA, Takotsubo

Standard revised to explicitly include Type 1 & 2 MI, MINOCA, and Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy.

G1: May resume driving 1 week after ACS if successful PCI & LVEF ‚â• 40%. If no PCI or LVEF < 40%, must wait 4 weeks.

G2: Must stop driving and notify DVLA. May relicense after 6 weeks if LVEF ‚â• 40% and a functional test is met.

Arrhythmias ‚ñº

General, ICD, Pacemakers

If an arrhythmia causes or is likely to cause incapacity (e.g., a blackout), the driver must not drive for at least 4 weeks until controlled.

If an ICD is implanted for sustained ventricular arrhythmia (secondary prevention), G1 drivers must stop driving for 6 months from the date of implant or most recent appropriate therapy.

Pacemaker & ICD ‚ñº

Implant, Box Change

Pacemaker Implant: G1 drivers must stop driving for 1 week. Must notify DVLA of implant.

ICD Implant: G1 drivers must stop driving for 6 months. Must notify DVLA.

Note: A Group 2 licence is permanently revoked for any ICD implant.

Heart Failure ‚ñº

NYHA Classes

NYHA Class I/II: May drive and need not notify DVLA if symptoms are stable.

NYHA Class III/IV: Must stop driving and notify DVLA.

Note: For Group 2 drivers, if left ventricular ejection fraction is less than 40%, this is a permanent bar to licensing.

Diabetes & Hypoglycaemia ‚ñº

Type 1, Type 2, Severe Hypo

Type 1 DM: G1 drivers must have adequate awareness of hypoglycaemia and no more than one episode of severe hypoglycaemia while awake in the last 12 months. Must notify DVLA.

Type 2 DM (on sulfonylureas/glinides): G1 drivers may continue to drive if no more than one episode of severe hypoglycaemia in 12 months. No need to notify DVLA unless clinical information suggests otherwise.

Severe Hypoglycaemia (non-diabetic) ‚ñº

e.g., post-bariatric surgery

If a driver experiences an episode of severe hypoglycaemia (e.g., post-bariatric surgery), they must stop driving and notify DVLA. The same principles of adequate awareness and frequency apply. Licensing is considered individually.

Severe Anxiety or Depression ‚ñº

Significant impairment or suicidal ideation

If there are significant memory/concentration problems, agitation, behavioural disturbance, or suicidal thoughts, the driver must not drive and must notify DVLA.

G1: May be relicensed after 3 months if well and stable. G2: May be relicensed after 6 months if well and stable.

Psychotic Disorder ‚ñº

Acute episode or established illness

Drivers must not drive during acute illness and must notify DVLA.

G1: May be relicensed after 3 months if well and stable, and they adhere to treatment. G2: Requires 12 months of stability.

Hypomania or Mania ‚ñº

Stable or unstable conditions

Drivers must not drive during any acute illness and must notify DVLA.

G1: May be relicensed after 3 months (stable) or 6 months (unstable). G2: Requires 12 months of stability in all cases.

Alcohol Misuse/Dependence ‚ñº

Persistent misuse, high-risk features

Persistent Misuse: Driver must not drive and must notify DVLA. Licence is refused until a minimum period of controlled drinking or abstinence has been achieved (6 months for G1, 1 year for G2).

High-Risk Features: G1 drivers must have 1 year of abstinence from alcohol. G2 drivers require 3 years of abstinence. Ongoing monitoring by DVLA is required for both.

Drug Misuse/Dependence ‚ñº

Cannabis, Opiates, Methadone Programmes

Persistent Misuse/Dependence: Drivers must not drive and must notify DVLA. Licence is refused until a minimum period of abstinence has been achieved.

Group 1: 6 months of abstinence for drugs like cannabis/amphetamines. 1 year for opiates, opioids, methadone.

Group 2: 1 year of abstinence for drugs like cannabis/amphetamines. 3 years for opiates, opioids, methadone.
